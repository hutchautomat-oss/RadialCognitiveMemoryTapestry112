## 1. Aerospace Attention/Alarm Philosophy
When an aircraft enters a non-normal but acceptable state (like a temporarily inoperative piece of equipment) without requiring pilot intervention, aerospace uses specific conventions to prevent desensitization and alert fatigue. [1, 2] 

* Use it (Rank 1): Alarm Inhibition by System/Phase
* The Convention: Modern aircraft (e.g., the Airbus A350 ECAM or Boeing 787 EICAS) suppress entire categories of warnings or downgrade them to status-only depending on the flight phase. If the landing gear is retracted, take-off configuration alarms are inhibited. [3] 
* Application to You: Implement dynamic filtering based on the constellation's current "focus state" or mode. If a brain is intentionally executing a pruning cycle, logs and invariants relevant to that pruning should be suppressed or dimmed. Let the operator choose their primary mode (e.g., Active Problem Solving, Passive Observation), and dynamically hide the noise. [4, 5] 
* Use it (Rank 2): Master Warning/Caution "Boxing" for Known-Degraded States
* The Convention: In Boeing EICAS, when an advisory or caution message is persistent (e.g., a known-degraded but acceptable system), the pilot can press an acknowledge button to "box" or clear the alert from the primary flashing attention-getter. It drops to the bottom of the stack on the Status Page, clearing the visual field. [4, 6] 
* Application to You: Handle your "red parity" invariant by forcing a manual "Acknowledge" sequence that physically relocates the warning. Remove it from the INVARIANTS strip—where it triggers fight-or-flight—and box it in a secondary "Known Issues" registry. The strip remains dark/clear until a new invariant fails.
* Cargo-Cult It: The "Dark Cockpit" Principle
* The Convention: In transport category aircraft, a perfectly operating system displays no lights; only abnormalities illuminate.
* Why it's cargo-cult for you: A "dark cockpit" relies on a pilot's intuitive, out-the-window visual map. In your interface, the operator relies purely on synthetic visual data. A completely "dark" 3D lattice and renderer would leave the operator blind about whether the system is idling or crashed.

## 2. Multi-Entity Situational Awareness
To monitor a constellation of many brains and their relationships, you must borrow from systems designed to represent dynamic environments, tracks, and engagement spaces. [7] 

* Use it (Rank 1): Speed Leaders and Vectors
* The Convention: In NATO STANAG 4420 / MIL-STD-2525 and naval Combat Information Centers (CIC), a moving contact has a line projecting from it called a "speed leader". The length represents speed; the direction indicates heading.
* Application to You: When looking at the constellation of brains, represent their trajectory through the epistemic tiers using directional vectors. If a brain is rapidly speculating, a "speed leader" pulling toward the speculative Dream rim immediately communicates the cognitive trajectory of the AI, rather than forcing the operator to read a log.
* Use it (Rank 2): The Stack & Hook
* The Convention: AEGIS Combat System consoles display dozens of tracks simultaneously. To prevent clutter, operators see basic, monochrome shapes that signify affiliation (friendly/hostile) and domain (air/surface). To see granular parameters, the operator "hooks" (clicks) the track, causing a secondary data readout to appear on a separate portion of the glass.
* Application to You: Do not try to display dense memory arrays (ontology, occupancy bars, renderer) for all 50 brains simultaneously. Instead, show the constellation as a graph of minimal nodes (e.g., small circles or icons). Let the user "hook" a specific brain node to pull its dense 3D lattice, SYNC CORE, and ONTOLOGY data into an overlay or side-panel.
* Use it (Rank 3): Pair/Association Lines
* The Convention: In multi-vehicle displays and radar scope readouts, related objects or entities engaged in an action are connected by thin, dashed association lines.
* Application to You: In your multi-brain constellation view, use dynamic, low-chroma lines to physically connect brains that are sharing data, collaborating on a thought, or have synchronous epistemic states.
* Cargo-Cult It: 1:1 Naval Realistic Icons
* The Convention: Some advanced systems attempt to replace basic shapes with literal 3D models or icons of ships and planes.
* Why it's cargo-cult for you: Using realistic avatars or rich icons causes visual clutter on dense tactical screens. Because your brains are highly abstract entities (memory substrates), stick to standardized NTDS-style geometric shapes where variation is based strictly on functional states (e.g., an idling brain, a loading brain, a desynced brain), not decorative flair.
